:::tip[In short]
Data storytelling means presenting data as a **story with a conclusion**, not a dump of charts. The key: understand the audience, carry **one message** per slide, highlight the main thing and remove everything else. Numbers without a narrative don't change decisions — and decisions are exactly why you're hired.
:::

## Why you need it

An analyst who "just exported the data" is worth less than one who delivers the conclusion so it gets acted on. Storytelling turns analysis into a decision — the skill that separates a middle from a junior.

## What data storytelling is

Three components (per Cole Knaflic, "Storytelling with Data"):

1. **Data** — a correct analysis (the foundation).
2. **Visuals** — the right chart, without [clutter](/en/06-visualization/01-principles/).
3. **Narrative** — what it means and what to do.

Without the third component you're left with just a chart; the value is in combining all three.

## Start with the audience and the message

Before building a chart, answer two questions:

- **Who's the audience?** A CEO wants the conclusion and an action in 10 seconds; an analyst team wants methodology and detail. The same analysis is presented differently.
- **What do I want them to do?** State one action/decision. If you can't — the analysis isn't ready to show.

## One chart — one message

:::caution[Don't dump 5 ideas on one slide]
Each chart should prove **one** statement, put in the title. Not "Metric dynamics", but "Revenue dropped due to KZ churn". The title is the conclusion, the chart is the proof. If a slide has several ideas — split it into several slides.
:::

## Annotations and highlighting

Don't make the viewer hunt for the point — show it:

- **Highlight** the key element with color, the rest in gray.
- **Label** the important point right on the chart ("promo launched here").
- An **arrow/line** to an anomaly or threshold.
- Remove everything that doesn't serve the slide's message.

## Presentation structure

A classic frame for an analysis report:

1. **Context** — what the question is and why it matters.
2. **The main conclusion — right away** (not at the end!). Busy people should grasp the point on the first slide.
3. **Evidence** — charts, one message each.
4. **Recommendation** — a concrete action.

This is the "Minto pyramid": conclusion first, details after for those who need them.

<details>
<summary>1. A slide title: "Revenue dynamics by region". What's wrong with it?</summary>

It's a description, not a conclusion. The title should carry the message the chart proves: e.g. "The 12% revenue drop is caused by churn in Kazakhstan". Then the audience grasps the point instantly, and the chart just confirms it.

</details>

<details>
<summary>2. You're presenting an analysis to a busy executive. Where to place the main conclusion?</summary>

At the very start, on the first slide. Executives don't watch through to "conclusions at the end". First the answer and recommendation, then evidence for those who want to dig in (the Minto pyramid principle).

</details>
