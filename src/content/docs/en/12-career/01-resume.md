:::tip[In short]
An analyst's resume sells **results in numbers**, not a list of duties. The main rule: instead of "made reports" — "automated reporting, cutting manual work by 8 hours a week". Tailor keywords to each posting (ATS systems filter by them), and put projects in a separate block if you're short on experience.
:::

## Why you need it

A resume is the entry filter: a recruiter looks at it for ~10 seconds, and an ATS robot often stands before them. A strong resume with numbers passes both and gets you to an interview. Technical skills don't work without the ability to present them.

## Structure

| Block | What's inside |
|-------|---------------|
| **Contacts** | name, city, email, links (GitHub, LinkedIn) |
| **Brief summary** | 2–3 lines: who you are and how you're useful |
| **Experience** | results in numbers (see STAR) |
| **Skills** | SQL, Python, BI — tailored to the posting's keywords |
| **Projects** | pet projects — especially important without experience |
| **Education** | university, courses |

Without experience, move the **Projects** block higher — it substitutes for the missing experience.

## STAR-style phrasing

Describe experience by result, not process. The STAR frame: Situation → Task → Action → **Result**.

- ❌ "Built dashboards in Tableau"
- ✅ "Built a funnel dashboard in Tableau that revealed a drop at the payment step; after the fix, conversion rose by 4%"

An action verb + what you did + the **measurable effect**.

## Numbers everywhere

:::tip[Numbers are the analyst's main language]
Try to express any achievement with a number: how much you sped up, how much money/hours you saved, how much a metric grew, what data volume you handled. "Optimized a SQL query from 40 to 3 minutes" weighs many times more than "optimized queries". No exact figure — give an estimate ("~", "about").
:::

## Passing ATS

An ATS (Applicant Tracking System) is a robot that filters out resumes before a human, by keywords.

- Insert exact terms from the posting (if they ask for "Power BI", write "Power BI", not "BI tools").
- A simple format: no image-tables, columns, or graphics over text — the robot can't read them.
- Standard section headings (Experience, Skills, Education).
- `.pdf` or `.docx` format, not an image.

## Common junior mistakes

:::caution[What to avoid]
- **A list of duties instead of results** — "did analytics" says nothing.
- **No numbers** — the main trouble; see above.
- **One resume for all postings** — doesn't match keywords, fails ATS.
- **Fluff and generic phrases** — "responsible, stress-resistant" without proof.
- **A 3-page wall** — for a junior one page is enough.
- **Listing 30 technologies** you "heard of" — they'll ask, and it'll be embarrassing.
:::

<details>
<summary>1. How to rewrite "Made reports for the sales department" into a strong line?</summary>

Add a result in numbers via STAR: e.g. "Automated the sales department's weekly reporting in Power BI, cutting manual prep from 6 hours to 30 minutes and removing manual-entry errors". An action verb + what you did + a measurable effect — instead of describing the process.

</details>

<details>
<summary>2. Why does one universal resume pass selection poorly?</summary>

Because of the ATS: the robot looks for the specific posting's keywords. If it says "Power BI and DAX" while you have a vague "BI tools", there's no match — the resume is filtered out before a human. For each posting, tailor the skill phrasing to its terms.

</details>
