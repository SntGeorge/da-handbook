:::tip[In short]
A behavioral interview tests **soft skills and "what it's like to work with you"**. Answer with the **STAR** method (Situation → Task → Action → Result) on concrete stories from experience, not generic words. Prepare 5–6 ready stories in advance — they'll cover almost any question. And be sure to ask the interviewer your own questions.
:::

## Why you need it

The technical side can be improved, but a "toxic" candidate or one who can't work in a team won't be hired even with strong SQL. The behavioral round is about trust and compatibility. It's often underrated and failed precisely due to lack of preparation.

## The STAR method

The same [STAR as in the resume](/en/12-career/01-resume/), but spoken and detailed:

- **Situation** — the context: what the situation was.
- **Task** — your task/role in it.
- **Action** — what specifically **you** did (not "we", but "I").
- **Result** — how it ended, ideally with a number and a takeaway.

A story without a Result sounds unfinished; it's the result that shows your value.

## Top typical questions

- Tell me about a **hard task** and how you solved it.
- Describe a **conflict** with a colleague/manager — how you resolved it.
- A time you **made a mistake** — what happened and what you learned.
- How you delivered an **unpopular conclusion** to the business / argued with a PM.
- An example of working under a **deadline** / with unclear requirements.
- Why you're leaving / why us.

:::tip[Prepare 5–6 stories in advance]
Most questions revolve around the same themes: a hard task, a conflict, a failure, a success, a deadline, influencing a decision. Prepare 5–6 real STAR stories — and combine them for the specific question. Improvising on the spot is almost always worse.
:::

## Stories about conflicts and failures

:::caution[You can't answer "I've never had any" about failure]
A question about a mistake/conflict tests **reflection**, not the absence of failures. "I never make mistakes" is a red flag (no growth or honesty). The right scheme: what happened → what **you** did wrong → how you fixed it → **what lesson you took and how you do it differently now**. Emphasis on the takeaway, not excuses or blaming others.
:::

## Questions for the interviewer

At the end they almost always ask "do you have any questions". **No questions is a bad signal** (no interest). Good ones:

- How is the analytics team structured and who will I work with?
- What tasks are there in the first 3 months? What does success in this role look like?
- What stack and processes (how are data-driven decisions made)?
- How is growth/development structured?

It's both an impression of you and real information — whether the place suits you.

## Practice tasks

<details>
<summary>1. "Tell me about your biggest failure." How to structure the answer?</summary>

By STAR with emphasis on the lesson: situation → what your mistake was (honestly, first-person) → how you fixed it → what you took away and how you do it differently now. You can't answer "I had no failures" — they test reflection and growth, not flawlessness. Don't blame others.

</details>

<details>
<summary>2. At the end they ask "any questions for us?". Can you say "no, all clear"?</summary>

Not advisable — it reads as a lack of interest. Prepare 2–3 questions about the team, tasks for the first months, the role's success criteria, the stack and processes. It improves the impression and gives you real information on whether the position suits you.

</details>
