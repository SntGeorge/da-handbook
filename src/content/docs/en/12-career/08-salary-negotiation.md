:::tip[In short]
An offer can almost always be improved — companies build in slack. Before negotiating: **learn the market range**, define your floor (**BATNA**) and look at **total compensation** (not just base salary). The main leverage is **multiple offers**. Negotiating politely is the norm, not impudence; a refusal rarely rescinds an offer.
:::

## Why you need it

The difference between "accepting the first number" and "calmly negotiating" is tens of percent of income for years to come. Many lose money simply because they're afraid to ask. The negotiation skill pays off instantly.

## Researching market ranges

Before talking money, find out what's actually paid for such a role/grade/region: [Glassdoor, Levels.fyi](/en/12-career/03-where-to-search/), salary surveys, acquaintances in the industry. Without a benchmark you name a number blindly and easily undersell or scare them off.

## BATNA

**BATNA** (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement) is your best fallback if you don't agree (another offer, your current job, the ability to wait). The stronger the BATNA, the more confident the negotiation: you have somewhere to go. Define your floor in advance — you don't go below it.

## Anchoring: who names a number first

:::caution[Don't name an exact number first at the screening]
On the early "what are your expectations?" question, an exact figure boxes you in: name too little and that's what you'll get; too much and you're filtered out. Tactics:

- Deflect: "what range is budgeted for this role?".
- Name a **range** based on research (with a lower bound no lower than your floor).
- Refer to total compensation, not the bare base.

When the offer is already on the table — conversely, you can anchor up with a justified ask.
:::

## Total compensation

Look at the **whole** package, not just the base:

| Component | What it is |
|-----------|------------|
| Base | fixed salary |
| Bonus | annual/quarterly, % of base |
| Equity / RSU | a share in the company (in tech/abroad) |
| Other | health insurance, learning, relocation, remote |

An offer with a lower base but a bonus and equity may be better. Compare whole packages.

## Multiple offers as leverage

The strongest argument. That's why it's worth running [several processes in parallel](/en/12-career/04-interview-process/) and trying to get offers to arrive close in time. A competing offer is an honest and powerful argument: "I have an offer at X, can you move?". Don't bluff with a nonexistent offer — it's easily checked and destroys trust.

## When and how to negotiate

- **Wait for the offer** — negotiating before it is premature.
- Thank them, express interest, then calmly: "can we discuss the compensation?".
- Justify: the market range, your skills/projects, another offer.
- Ask specifically and with justification, stay polite. A refusal to improve ≠ rescinding the offer — the worst case is almost always "we'll leave it as is".

<details>
<summary>1. At the HR screening they immediately ask for exact salary expectations. The best move?</summary>

Don't name an exact figure first: it anchors and risks underselling or being filtered out. Better to ask the role's budgeted range, or name a range based on research (lower bound ≥ your floor) and refer to total compensation. Exact negotiation is for when the offer is already on the table.

</details>

<details>
<summary>2. You got an offer. Should you negotiate if you fear it'll be rescinded?</summary>

Yes — polite negotiation is the norm, companies build in slack, and a refusal to raise almost never means rescinding (worst case — "we'll leave it as is"). Justify with the market range, skills and, if you have one, a competing offer. The real risk of losing an offer over a polite compensation question is minimal.

</details>
